\textbf{Equivalance}

The object of interest is the coefficient on the contemporaneous regressor.
Partialling the $p$ lags out of $y_t$ leaves the one-step reduced-form
innovation $u_t = y_t - \mathbb{E}\!\left[y_t \mid y_{t-1}, \ldots,
y_{t-p}\right]$, so by the Frisch--Waugh--Lovell theorem $\Phi_h$ equals the population projection coefficient of $y_{t+h}$ on $u_t$. Inserting the Wold representation \eqref{eq:wold_yt}, $y_{t+h} = \sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty}
\Psi_\ell\, u_{t+h-\ell}$, and using that $u_t$ is orthogonal to every
$u_{t+h-\ell}$ with $\ell \neq h$, the projection isolates a single term,
%
\begin{equation}
    \Phi_h = \mathbb{E}\!\left[y_{t+h}\, u_t'\right]
             \bigl(\mathbb{E}\!\left[u_t u_t'\right]\bigr)^{-1}
           = \Psi_h,
    \qquad \Phi_0 = \Psi_0 = I_K.
    \label{eq:lp_psi}
\end{equation}



Structural identification proceeds exactly as in the VAR case and is
logically distinct from the choice of estimator
\parencite{plagborg2021local}. Any identification scheme selects the
structural shock as a fixed linear combination of the reduced-form
innovations, $\varepsilon_{j,t} = \bigl(B^{-1}\bigr)_{j\cdot}\, u_t$
\parencite[p.~967]{plagborg2021local}, so the structural response is obtained
by post-multiplying the reduced-form responses by the impact matrix $B$,
%
\begin{equation}
    \theta_h^{ij} = [\Phi_h B]_{ij} = [\Psi_h B]_{ij}
                  = e_i'\, \Psi_h\, B\, e_j,
    \label{eq:lp_sirf}
\end{equation}
%
which is the same estimand as \eqref{eq:sirf}. Under recursive
(Cholesky) identification $B$ is the lower-triangular factor of $\Sigma_u$,
and \eqref{eq:lp_sirf} can equivalently be obtained in a single step by
ordering the variables and regressing $y_{i,t+h}$ on the contemporaneous
value of the shock-bearing variable, controlling for the variables ordered
before it together with the lags \parencites{jorda2005estimation}{plagborg2021local}.
 
The same construction accommodates the remaining standard schemes. Recursive, long-run, and sign restrictions are each implementable through the projection \eqref{eq:lp_reduced} because all of them merely fix the vector $b$ that maps the Wold innovations into the shock of interest
\parencite[p.~967]{plagborg2021local}. When an external instrument or proxy for the shock is available, ordering the instrument first in the conditioning set---the ``internal instrument'' approach---identifies the relative structural responses even when the shock is noninvertible, whereas the external SVAR-IV/LP-IV implementation of \textcite{stock2018identification} requires invertibility \parencite[p.~973]{plagborg2021local}. Because identification operates on the population innovations rather than on the estimator, any scheme available to the VAR can be carried out with local projections and vice versa \parencite[p.~975]{plagborg2021local}.
 
Each regression in \eqref{eq:lp_reduced} is estimated equation-by-equation by OLS, and the structural estimator $\hat{\theta}_h^{ij} = e_i'\, \hat{\Phi}_h\, \hat{B}\, e_j$ is, like its VAR counterpart, a smooth function of the first-stage coefficients and the residual covariance. Because the projection coefficient \emph{is} the impulse response, no companion-matrix recursion intervenes between estimation and the reported response
\parencite[p.~65]{jorda2025local}.

regression rather than from the recursion $\hat{\Psi}_h = \sum_{\ell=1}^{\min(h,p)}

To see this, recall that under the recursive identification of 
Section~\ref{sec:var} the local projection in Equation~\eqref{eq:lp_shock} regresses 
$y_{t+h}$ on the structural shock $\varepsilon_{1,t}$, which has unit variance and is 
orthogonal to the lagged controls. The lagged controls therefore drop out of the 
projection coefficient, leaving
\begin{equation}
\beta_h = \operatorname{Cov}\!\big(y_{t+h},\, \varepsilon_{1,t}\big).
\end{equation}
From the structural moving-average representation 
$y_t = \sum_{\ell \geq 0}\Theta_\ell\,\varepsilon_{t-\ell}$ with 
$\Theta_\ell = \Psi_\ell B$, the outcome at $t+h$ loads on the time-$t$ shock only 
through the horizon-$h$ coefficient, so that
\begin{equation}
\beta_h = \operatorname{Cov}\!\big(y_{t+h},\, \varepsilon_{1,t}\big) 
        = \Theta_h e_1 = \Psi_h B e_1 = \theta_h,
\end{equation}
which is exactly the structural impulse response of Equation~(7). The 
local-projection and vector-autoregressive estimands therefore coincide in 
population.

\textbf{METHODS:} We compare two classes of estimators. The first is a fixed 
LP(4) estimator, which serves as the reference point. At each horizon 
$h = 1, \ldots, H$, the LP(4) estimator is obtained from the OLS regression

\begin{equation}
    y_{1,t+h} = \mu_h + \beta_h x_t + \sum_{\ell=1}^{4} \delta_{h,\ell}' 
    y_{t-\ell} + \xi_{h,t}, \quad h = 1, \ldots, H
\end{equation}

where $x_t = e_1'(\hat{B}^{(4)})^{-1}\hat{u}_t^{(4)}$ is the estimated 
structural shock recovered from the first-stage VAR(4). The structural 
impulse response estimate at horizon $h$ is $\hat{\theta}_h^{\mathrm{LP}} 
= \hat{\beta}_h$.

The second class consists of a sweep of VAR estimators of increasing order, 
$\mathrm{VAR}(q)$ for $q = 1, \ldots, p + H - 1$, where $p = 4$ and $H = 20$. 
For each order $q$, the VAR is estimated equation-by-equation via OLS, the 
reduced-form covariance matrix $\hat{\Sigma}_u^{(q)}$ is Cholesky-decomposed 
to recover the structural impact matrix $\hat{B}^{(q)}$, and the $h$-step 
ahead structural IRF is computed as

\begin{equation}
    \hat{\theta}_h^{\mathrm{VAR}(q)} = \hat{\Psi}_h^{(q)} \hat{B}^{(q)} e_1
\end{equation}

where $\hat{\Psi}_h^{(q)}$ denotes the $h$-step ahead moving-average matrix 
of the estimated $\mathrm{VAR}(q)$. This sweep constitutes the 
complexity-adjusted benchmark motivated by \textcite{ludwig2026local}: by 
Theorem~1 of that paper, LP(4) at horizon $h$ is algebraically equivalent to 
a sequence of VAR predictions stepping through orders $4, 5, \ldots, 4 + h - 1$. 
The fair comparison is therefore not LP(4) against VAR(4) alone, but against 
the full range of VAR specifications up to order $p + H - 1$. Note that this 
equivalence holds exactly for the sequence of VARs of increasing order; 
comparing LP(4) to any single VAR$(q^*)$ is an approximation, with the 
complexity-adjusted single-order approximation given by $q^* = p + 
\frac{h-1}{2}$ \parencite{ludwig2026local}. The equal-lag VAR(4) is nested 
in the sweep as a special case and is retained as an additional reference 
point to connect to the conventional benchmark in the literature.

Identification of the structural shock is implemented as follows. For each 
$\mathrm{VAR}(q)$ in the sweep, the structural impact matrix $\hat{B}^{(q)}$ 
is obtained via Cholesky decomposition of the order-specific residual 
covariance matrix $\hat{\Sigma}_u^{(q)}$. For the LP(4), which does not 
estimate a complete system, identification is achieved through a first-stage 
VAR(4): the structural shock is constructed as $\hat{\varepsilon}_{1t} = 
e_1'(\hat{B}^{(4)})^{-1}\hat{u}_t^{(4)}$, consistent with the LP lag order 
$p = 4$. Since \textcite{plagborg2021local} establish that LP and VAR share 
the same implicit shock in population under recursive identification, this 
ensures that any divergence in estimated IRFs reflects differences in 
propagation rather than identification. No data-dependent lag selection is 
performed within the sweep; all VAR orders $q = 1, \ldots, p + H - 1$ are 
estimated on each simulated sample, ensuring that the comparison is across 
fixed specifications rather than across selection procedures.

\textbf{PERFORMANCE:} Performance is assessed along two dimensions, reported 
horizon-by-horizon for $h = 1, \ldots, 20$. For point estimation, we report 
bias, variance, MSE, and RMSE of the IRF estimates $\hat{\theta}_h$ relative 
to the true estimand $\theta_h$, computed across $B = 5{,}000$ Monte Carlo 
replications in line with the number of replications used by 
\textcite{li2024local}.

Monte Carlo standard errors are reported alongside all point-estimator 
performance measures to quantify simulation uncertainty, with 
$\mathrm{MCSE}(\widehat{\mathrm{Bias}}_h) = \sqrt{\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}_h 
/ B}$.

For inference, we assess coverage of nominal $95\%$ confidence intervals. 
For the VAR$(q)$ sweep, confidence intervals are constructed via the delta 
method, propagating estimation uncertainty in the reduced-form coefficients 
$(\hat{A}_1, \ldots, \hat{A}_q)$ through the nonlinear mapping to structural 
IRFs $\hat{\theta}_h^{\mathrm{VAR}(q)} = \hat{\Psi}_h^{(q)} \hat{B}^{(q)} 
e_1$ \parencite{kilian2017structural}. For LP(4), confidence intervals are 
based on heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, which suffice under the 
martingale difference assumption on the structural shock 
\parencite{plagborg2021local}. Coverage is defined as the fraction of 
replications in which the nominal $95\%$ interval contains the true estimand 
$\theta_h$:

\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\mathrm{Coverage}}_h = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b=1}^{B} 
    \mathbf{1}\left[\theta_h \in \hat{CI}_h^{(b)}\right]
\end{equation}

that horizon, which is clearest atc larger sample sizes where the frontier flattens and noise is reduced.

Figure \ref{fig:BL_COV} shows the empirical coverage of $95\%$ confidence intervals alongside a 
validation of the VAR(4) standard error. The left column shows that LP(4) holds its nominal level closely 
at low and mid persistence and only drifts downward at high persistence, remaining the most reliable of the 
three throughout. VAR(4) behaves very differently across regimes. At low persistence it bends upward and 
reaches full coverage by about horizon seven. Instead of signaling outstanding performance, this effect is mechanical in nature, 
as the response has decayed to near zero by then and the interval almost always brackets the near-zero truth. 
At mid persistence VAR(4) falls just below LP(4) with a slight downward slope, while at high persistence it 
declines steeply and under-covers across the whole window. The over-parameterised VAR(23) lies above LP(4) 
at every persistence level, since its many estimated lags inflate the interval width and leave it conservative.

The right column explains the VAR(4) pattern by plotting the delta-method standard error against the empirical 
sampling dispersion of the estimator. At low and mid persistence the two lie almost on top of one another, 
which indicates that the delta-method interval is correctly sized and that the coverage distortions reflect the 
shape of the response rather than a faulty standard error. At high persistence the picture breaks, and the 
empirical dispersion shifts above the delta-method standard error. The two paths still exhibit the same shape, 
so the interval is systematically too narrow and VAR(4) under-covers. 

As the persistence of the process rises toward the unit root, the coefficient estimates become increasingly biased 
and skewed. As the delta-method process assumes well-centered estimates \parencite{kilian1998small}, the skewness 
leaves the standard error it returns understating the estimator's true variability. The right column shows exactly 
this process, as the resulting narrow interval and downward bias cause under-coverage.
The distortion is therefore a property of the delta-method standard 
error rather than of the VAR(4) estimator itself, which remains efficient even where its analytical interval is 
unreliable.

Extension~1 examines 
whether the finite-sample MSE ranking between LP(4) and the complexity-adjusted 
VAR sweep is robust to dynamic misspecification of the Wold representation. The 
baseline VAR(4) is augmented by a first-order moving average component, yielding 
a VARMA(4,1) DGP:

\begin{equation}
    y_t = A_1 y_{t-1} + A_2 y_{t-2} + A_3 y_{t-3} + A_4 y_{t-4} 
    + u_t + \Theta u_{t-1}, \quad u_t = B\varepsilon_t, \quad 
    \varepsilon_t \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0, I_2)
\end{equation}

where $\Theta = \theta I_2$ is a scalar-scaled MA impact matrix, with 
misspecification strength $\theta \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.6\}$. The case $\theta = 0$ 
nests back to the baseline VAR(4), ensuring continuity across designs. The VAR 
coefficient matrices $A_1, \ldots, A_4$ and the impact matrix $B$ are inherited 
from the baseline DGP, so that all variation across Extension~1 scenarios is 
attributable exclusively to the MA component.

The VARMA(4,1) DGP is invertible for all $\theta \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.6\}$, 
admitting an infinite-order VAR representation. This means the complexity-adjusted 
VAR sweep retains its theoretical motivation: higher-order VAR$(q)$ specifications 
progressively approximate the true dynamics as $q \to \infty$, though at the cost 
of increasing parameter proliferation in finite samples. The central question is 
therefore whether LP(4) --- which is robust to this misspecification by virtue of 
its zero asymptotic bias \parencite{olea2025local} --- retains its MSE 
advantage over the equal-lag VAR(4), and where on the complexity-adjusted VAR 
frontier it sits relative to the baseline.

Persistence is varied across $\rho \in (0.5, 0.95)$ as in the baseline, 
since the degree to which the MA component distorts finite-sample VAR 
approximations depends directly on the persistence of the underlying process: 
at high $\rho$, shocks decay slowly and the MA term remains influential at 
longer horizons, amplifying the VAR's approximation bias. Sample sizes follow 
the baseline design, $T \in \{100, 200, 240, 500\}$. The misspecification 
strength $\theta \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.6\}$ is thus varied jointly with 
persistence and sample size, yielding a three-way design that isolates the 
marginal contribution of MA misspecification from the persistence and 
finite-sample channels already documented in the baseline.

The structural estimand $\theta_h = \Psi_h^{\mathrm{VARMA}} B e_1$ is defined 
with respect to the true VARMA moving-average matrices $\Psi_h^{\mathrm{VARMA}}$, 
computed analytically from the VARMA companion representation prior to each 
simulation run. Both estimators --- LP(4) and the VAR$(q)$ sweep --- are applied 
without modification to the VARMA-generated data, so that the misspecification 
is fully absorbed into the finite-sample performance of each estimator rather 
than accommodated by the estimation procedure. Performance measures are identical 
to the baseline.

Figure~\ref{fig:DYN_MISSPEC_IRF} characterises the true structural impulse response together 
with the residual structure that the moving-average component induces. The left panel of 
each subfigure plots the true estimand $\theta_h$ across misspecification strengths. 
The responses retain the decaying profile of the baseline and become more sharply L-shaped 
as persistence rises. Because the moving-average term carries the lagged innovation 
$\Theta u_{t-1}$ into the current observation, shocks raise the response for an extra period 
beyond the autoregressive propagation. As $\theta$ grows the impulse response turns increasingly 
hump-shaped, and at high persistence with strong misspecification it is no longer monotone 
and peaks at (h = 1) before decaying. Because the estimand is genuinely $\theta$-dependent, 
the moving-average component is a persistent feature of the response.

The middle and right panels diagnose the residual structure that the moving-average term leaves 
behind. They plot the autocorrelation of the squared and level residuals of a VAR(4) fitted to 
the VARMA data against the $\pm 1.96/\sqrt{N}$ white-noise band. The squared-residual 
autocorrelations stay inside the band at every $\theta$ and $\rho$, so the shock variance is 
well behaved and shows no clustering. The level residuals tell a different story, as they are white 
at $\theta = 0$, but as $\theta$ grows, a negative spike at lag four and a positive spike at lag 
five appear. This is the alternating echo that an invertible MA(1) leaves in a four-lag fit, 
since its autoregressive representation has geometrically decaying, sign-alternating weights 
that the limited lags of VAR cannot reproduce. 

The strength of this echo depends on persistence, as at $\rho = 0.5$ and $\rho = 0.7$ both spikes 
clear the band, whereas at $\rho = 0.95$ the lag-five spike falls back inside the band. 
Higher persistence scales up the autoregressive 
coefficients and makes the four included lags jointly more informative about the current value, 
so the more distant part of the echo is increasingly absorbed by the fit and only the lag-four 
spike remains significant.

The variance panel reverses the picture, as the estimators line up by lag-length, 
with variance rising with VAR order. LP(4) sits among the higher-variance VAR
models, since its direct horizon-by-horizon regressions are less efficient 
than the iterated VAR estimates. Crucially, VAR(2) is not an outlier in this panel, as its 
parsimony makes it one of the lowest-variance estimators.

The MSE and RMSE panels combine these two channels through $\mathrm{MSE} = \mathrm{Bias}^2 + 
\mathrm{Var}$, which visualizes the cost of too low lag counts. VAR(2) jumps back to the top, 
far above every other estimator, as its squared bias dominates its low variance. 
The longer-lagged VARs behave in the opposite way, as they keep the small bias of LP(4) 
while paying less variance. Thus, VAR(4) and VAR(8) sit below LP(4) across 
the whole horizon window. 

On root mean squared error the equal-lag VAR therefore retains an efficiency advantage over 
LP(4) even under dynamic misspecification. This advantage grows with persistence, as the 
curvature of every profile increases. The contrasting results demosntrate how VAR(2) is an 
outlier in bias, MSE, and RMSE but not in variance, 

This finds its failure to originate from the wrong-estimand effect, caused by under-lagging. The 
same logic explains why the relevant comparison is not LP(4) against any single VAR, but LP(4) 
against the whole order sweep. A badly chosen low order can dominate the error budget 
through bias alone, while a well-chosen one keeps both ingredients small.


% ----------------------------------- Coverage OLD

Figure~\ref{fig:DYN_MISSPEC_COVERAGE} turns to inference by visualizing the coverage of 
nominal $95\%$ confidence intervals over horizons $h = 1$ to $20$ at $T = 250$. Each subfigure 
contains three panels. The left and middle panels show the coverage of the VAR(4) and LP(4) 
intervals with one curve per misspecification strength $\theta$, and the right panel checks the 
VAR(4) delta-method standard error against the empirical sampling standard deviation of 
$\hat\theta_h$. The striking feature is that coverage is governed by persistence rather than by 
the misspecification, as the four $\theta$ curves lie almost on top of one another within each panel, 
while their level of coverage shifts substantially as $\rho$ moves across the three regimes.

For VAR(4), whose intervals are calculated using the delta method, the persistence pattern mirrors the 
baseline findings. At $\rho = 0.5$ the intervals over-cover, rising toward full coverage at longer 
horizons. At $\rho = 0.7$ they sit a little below 
nominal, and at $\rho = 0.95$ they under-cover and declinine with increasing horizons.
LP(4), which uses heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, is far steadier and holds near $0.95$ at low and intermediate persistence.
It slips only to about $0.89$ at $\rho = 0.95$, so it stays closer to the nominal target than VAR(4) throughout.
Still, as we move closer to the unit root it shows similiar erosion of performance with increasing horizons.

The effect of the moving-average term itself is second order, as $\rho = 0.5$ shows VAR(4) coverage 
drifting down only slightly as $\theta$ grows. At $\rho = 0.7$ it moves the other way and improves from about $0.89$ at $\theta = 0$ to 
about $0.93$ at $\theta = 0.6$. Paradoxically, this means that the correctly specified case is the worst rather than the 
best. At $\rho = 0.95$ the curves are essentially flat across $\theta$, which rules out any 
simple story in which stronger misspecification steadily erodes coverage. The intermediate-
persistence improvement is best read as a coincidental interaction between the lifted short-
horizon response and the delta-method standard error rather than as a genuine benefit of 
misspecification. We can thus conservatively estimate that the moving-average term leaves coverage largely 
unchanged at this sample size.

The right panel explains the persistence patterns, as there is almost no deviation between SE's and SD's in $\rho = 0.5$ and $\rho = 0.7$.
Thus, the analytical formula is accurate and the coverage behaviour is not an artefact of a broken standard 
error. At $\rho = 0.95$ a gap opens, with the empirical dispersion sitting above the delta-method 
standard error over short and intermediate horizons. The first-order delta-method approximation 
understates the true sampling variability of the response near the unit root, which causes the intervals to 
be too narrow, leading to under-coverage in VAR(4). A similiar behavior was described in the base-line case, 
confirming that persistence and not the moving-average misspecification drive the coverage results.



Taken together, our simulation did not result in evidence that introducing a moving-average 
component would overturn the findings established in the baseline. At sample sizes typical of 
applied work, around $T = 250$, the equal-lag VAR(4) 
stays the more efficient point estimator while LP(4) offers no clear inferential advantage in 
return. The moving-average term lifts the true response at short horizons but leaves its overall 
decaying shape intact. In a fitted VAR(4) it shows up as serial correlation in the residuals 
and not as changing variance, so it distorts the dynamics of the process rather than the 
size of its shocks.

On point estimation the equal-lag VAR(4) 
shares the small bias of LP(4) at $T = 250$ and pays less variance, so it keeps a root mean 
squared error advantage across all three persistence regimes. The only estimator that 
fails is the under-lagged VAR(2), whose error is dominated by bias rather than noise. On inference 
the picture is driven by persistence rather than by the misspecification, since the four $\theta$ 
curves nearly coincide while coverage moves from over-covering at $\rho = 0.5$ to marked 
under-covering at $\rho = 0.95$, where the delta-method standard error understates the true 
dispersion. LP(4) stays closer to nominal throughout, but erodes near the unit root as well, so 
its robustness is only partial. The moving-average distortion is therefore real and visible in 
the residuals, yet at $T = 250$ it remains too small relative to estimation variance to change the 
baseline verdict that VAR is the more efficient estimator, while neither estimator delivers 
reliable inference at high persistence.
