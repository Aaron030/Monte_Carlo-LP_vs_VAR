\section{Introduction}
Impulse responses to exogenous shocks are estimated through two main approaches, 
vector autoregressive (VAR) and local projection (LP) models. Since the introduction 
of the latter, an extensive literature has compared the two. One strand establishes 
that, under relatively general conditions, LPs and VARs estimate the same population 
impulse responses \parencite{plagborg2021local, fusari2026more}. In finite samples at 
equal lag lengths, however, LPs were often viewed as less sensitive to misspecification 
\parencite{jorda2005estimation, ramey2016macroeconomic}, a property that 
\textcite{olea2024double} subsequently formalise, whereas VARs were seen as more 
statistically efficient \parencite{li2024local, olea2025local}. \textcite{ludwig2026local} 
explains this finite-sample bias-variance tradeoff analytically, showing that an LP can be 
represented as a sequence of VAR estimations of increasing lag length as the horizon grows, 
and therefore argues for comparing LPs and VARs by effective complexity rather than equal lag length.

Our paper asks two questions. First, under correct specification, where does a fixed LP 
estimator sit relative to the full complexity-adjusted VAR frontier, and does the conventional 
equal-lag ranking survive once LP(4) is compared against the entire 
VAR-order frontier rather than only the equal-lag VAR(4)? To answer this, we use Monte Carlo 
simulation to benchmark a fixed LP(4) against progressively higher-order VAR specifications 
\parencite{de2025long} across empirically relevant sample sizes \parencite[see e.g.][]{herbst2024bias}. 
Second, once controlled dynamic misspecification is introduced, does any genuine LP advantage 
emerge, and does it manifest in point estimation, in inference, or both? Here we augment 
the baseline VAR(4) with a moving-average component, yielding a VARMA(4,1) DGP, and track 
how LP(4), the equal-lag VAR(4), and higher-order VARs respond as the degree of 
misspecification increases. This is the setting in which local projections are conventionally 
expected to dominate, as the conjectured robustness of the direct projection should bind 
precisely where the iterated VAR is misspecified \parencite{jorda2005estimation, li2024local,olea2024double}.

Our baseline findings follow the established notion that under correct specification the conventional point-estimation advantage of equal-lag VARs over LPs persists, but it is largely an artifact of mismatched model complexity. A fixed LP(4) already operates like a highly parameterized system, sitting high on the complexity-adjusted VAR frontier (beyond $q \approx 15$). This low-order efficiency comes at a cost in inference, as VAR(4) coverage deteriorates sharply near the unit-root boundary while local projections hold near-nominal coverage across persistence regimes. Introducing dynamic misspecification through a VARMA(4,1)process does not overturn the point-estimation ranking, since the equal-lag VAR(4) still strictly dominates LP(4) on root mean squared error. It instead sharpens the divergence in inference between the equal-lag models, where local projections retain their decisive advantage. However, this disparity is strictly a function of the lag restriction. Once the local projection is evaluated against appropriately higher-order VARs on the complexity frontier, the finite-sample gap systematically closes. A sufficiently flexible VAR successfully absorbs the unmodeled moving-average dynamics, catching up to the LP(4) in terms of bias reduction and matching its robust coverage levels, even near the non-stationary boundary.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 reviews vector autoregressions 
and local projections, the empirical literature on their bias-variance trade-off at equal 
lag lengths, and their asymptotic and finite-sample equivalence. Section 3 presents the 
baseline simulation study under correct specification, Section 4 introduces dynamic 
misspecification, and Section 5 concludes.
