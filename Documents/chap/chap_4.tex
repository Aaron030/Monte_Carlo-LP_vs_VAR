\section{Extension 1: Dynamic Misspecification}

The baseline established a clear reference under correct specification. The equal-lag VAR is the 
more efficient point estimator, while the local projection offers more reliable inference. 
Whether that robustness argument for local projections survives once the assumption of correct 
specification is relaxed remains open, and this extension addresses it directly by introducing 
controlled dynamic misspecification, targeting the bias channel where the complexity adjustment 
theoretically binds.

\subsection{Simulation Design}

The structural estimand, the 
recursive Cholesky identification, and all performance measures, including the 
$B = 5{,}000$ replication count, are inherited unchanged from the baseline, while the baseline 
$\text{VAR}(4)$ is augmented by a first-order moving-average component to yield a 
$\text{VARMA}(4,1)$ DGP of the following form,


\begin{equation}
    y_t = A_1 y_{t-1} + A_2 y_{t-2} + A_3 y_{t-3} + A_4 y_{t-4} 
    + u_t + \Theta u_{t-1}, \quad u_t = B\varepsilon_t, \quad 
    \varepsilon_t \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, I_2),
\end{equation}

where $\Theta = \tau I_2$ is a scalar-scaled MA impact matrix on the lagged reduced-form 
innovation $u_{t-1}$, with misspecification strength $\tau \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.6\}$ and $\tau = 0$ 
recovering the baseline $\text{VAR}(4)$. The autoregressive matrices $A_1, \ldots, A_4$ and the 
impact matrix $B$ are inherited from the baseline, so all variation across scenarios comes from 
the MA component.

The $\text{VARMA}(4,1)$ is invertible for all $\tau \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.6\}$, since the roots of 
$\det(I_2 + \Theta z)$ lie outside the unit circle, so the process admits an infinite-order VAR 
representation. The misspecification is therefore approximable. A higher-order $\text{VAR}(q)$ 
recovers the true dynamics as $q \to \infty$, whereas the equal-lag $\text{VAR}(4)$ carries a 
truncation bias that does not vanish, and the $\text{LP}(4)$ stays consistent by virtue of its 
zero asymptotic bias \parencite{olea2025local}.

Persistence is again varied across $\rho \in \{0.5, 0.7, 0.95\}$ via the spectral radius of the 
companion matrix, which matters here as the MA distortion to the finite-sample VAR 
approximation grows with persistence. At high $\rho$ shocks decay slowly, so the MA term stays 
influential at longer horizons and amplifies the approximation bias. To isolate this MA channel 
from the finite-sample effects already documented in the baseline, the sample size is fixed at 
$T = 250$ after a 200-period burn-in, and $\tau$ is varied jointly with persistence in a two-way 
design.

The structural estimand keeps the baseline form $\theta_h = \Psi_h B e_1$ but is now built from 
the true VARMA Wold matrices $\Psi_h^{\mathrm{VARMA}}$, which solve $A(L)\Psi(L) = I_2 + \Theta L$ 
and are computed analytically before each run via
\begin{equation}
    \Psi_0 = I_2, \qquad 
    \Psi_h = \sum_{i=1}^{\min(h,4)} A_i \Psi_{h-i} + \Theta_h, 
    \qquad \Theta_h = \begin{cases} \Theta & h = 1 \\ \mathbf{0} & h > 1 \end{cases}
\end{equation}
so that the additive MA term $\Theta_h$ enters only at horizon $h = 1$. Both estimators are 
applied without modification to the VARMA data, so the misspecification is absorbed into their 
finite-sample performance rather than accommodated by the estimation. The complexity frontier 
aggregates root mean squared error across the full grid of $\tau$, while the horizon-by-horizon 
bias, variance, and mean squared error decomposition is reported only at the strongest 
misspecification $\tau = 0.6$, which isolates the approximation error at its most pronounced and 
serves as a stress test of the estimator ranking.

\subsection{Simulation Results}

Figure \ref{fig:DYN_MISSPEC_COMPLEXITY} traces the horizon-averaged root mean squared error 
against VAR order $q$, one curve per misspecification strength $\tau$ with $\text{LP}(4)$ as a 
horizontal reference. Two level effects stand out. Errors rise with the misspecification $\tau$, 
which lifts the whole frontier and its $\text{LP}(4)$ line, and with persistence, which also 
spreads the $\tau$ curves apart, from a narrow band at $\rho=0.5$ to a wide one near the 
unit-root boundary. Throughout, the equal-lag $\text{VAR}(4)$ stays below its $\text{LP}(4)$ 
line, so misspecification does not overturn the baseline point-estimation ranking.

What misspecification changes is LP's position on the frontier. The optimal VAR order stays low, 
near $\text{VAR}(1)$ to $\text{VAR}(2)$ and drifting up only at the most extreme $\rho$ and 
$\tau$. While the rising frontier crosses the $\text{LP}(4)$ line only at high orders, this crossing 
moves steadily leftward as $\tau$ grows. At $\rho=0.5$, the intersection falls from $q=19$ under 
correct specification ($\tau=0$) to approximately $q=18$ at intermediate misspecification levels, 
settling exactly at $q=18$ for $\tau=0.6$. This leftward drift is mirrored at intermediate 
persistence ($\rho=0.7$), where the intersection decreases from $q=18$ to roughly $q=17$. Near 
the unit root boundary ($\rho=0.95$), the equivalence occurs at generally lower lag orders across 
the board, shifting from approximately $q=17$ at $\tau=0$ down to $q=16$ at the strongest 
misspecification level. Consequently, $\text{LP}(4)$ becomes equivalent to an increasingly 
parsimonious VAR even as its absolute error rises. The low-order models bear the cost of stronger 
misspecification, most visibly at $\rho=0.95$ and $\tau=0.6$, where $\text{VAR}(2)$ spikes into a 
jagged low-order end while staying below the heavily parameterized $\text{VAR}(23)$ at the far right.

Figure \ref{fig:DYN_MISSPEC_POINT_ESTIMATION} reports bias, variance, MSE, and RMSE at the 
strongest misspecification $\tau=0.6$ and $T=250$, horizon by horizon across the three 
persistence regimes, with $\text{LP}(4)$ against a representative VAR set. The picture is 
qualitatively the baseline's, scaled up by the misspecification, and as persistence rises the 
levels grow and the horizon at which the estimators diverge moves later.

Both bias and variance are ordered by lag length. In bias, $\text{LP}(4)$ settles to a small, 
roughly constant negative value, the under-parameterized $\text{VAR}(2)$ is the most biased but 
sheds it fastest, and higher orders track the $\text{LP}(4)$ path progressively longer, with 
$\text{VAR}(16)$ and $\text{VAR}(23)$ following it almost throughout. Variance is the mirror 
image. The low-order VARs collapse toward zero earliest, the heavily parameterized 
$\text{VAR}(16)$ and $\text{VAR}(23)$ overshoot the $\text{LP}(4)$ variance before matching it 
at longer horizons, and $\text{LP}(4)$ itself stays comparatively stable. Root mean squared 
error follows variance, except for a short-horizon $\text{VAR}(2)$ spike that carries through 
its bias.

Higher persistence scales this pattern up rather than changing it. At $\rho=0.7$ the same 
ordering holds with larger absolute bias and a shallower variance decline, and near the 
unit-root boundary at $\rho=0.95$ everything is elevated, $\text{VAR}(2)$ turns erratic, the 
remaining VARs and $\text{LP}(4)$ share a common downward path, and the root mean squared error 
takes the same arched profile as the baseline, scaled up.

Figure \ref{fig:DYN_MISSPEC_COVERAGE} reports the empirical coverage of nominal 95 percent 
confidence intervals over $h = 1$ to $20$ at the strongest misspecification $\tau = 0.6$ and 
$T = 250$, for the equal-lag $\text{VAR}(4)$, the high-order $\text{VAR}(23)$, and the 
$\text{LP}(4)$, alongside the $\text{VAR}(4)$ delta-method standard error against its empirical 
sampling standard deviation. The baseline coverage ranking survives the misspecification. The 
$\text{LP}(4)$ stays near nominal except for a mild slip near the unit root, while the 
$\text{VAR}(4)$ delta-method intervals remain miscalibrated and strongly persistence-sensitive.

Persistence, not the misspecification, drives the differences. The $\text{LP}(4)$ is the most 
stable throughout, holding around $0.95$ at low and intermediate persistence and slipping only 
to about $0.88$ at $\rho = 0.95$. The $\text{VAR}(4)$ instead swings with persistence, 
over-covering at $\rho = 0.5$ as it climbs to full coverage and under-covering severely at 
$\rho = 0.95$, where it falls monotonically to roughly $0.74$. The high-order $\text{VAR}(23)$ 
sits between the two, drifting above nominal at low persistence and tracking the $\text{LP}(4)$ 
near $0.90$ at the boundary.

The right column locates the cause. At $\rho = 0.5$ and $\rho = 0.7$ the $\text{VAR}(4)$ 
delta-method standard error and the empirical sampling standard deviation are nearly 
indistinguishable, so the miscalibration there reflects a structural feature of the response 
rather than a broken standard error. At $\rho = 0.95$ a persistent gap opens, with the empirical 
dispersion sitting above the analytical standard error, and unlike the baseline it does not 
close at long horizons, mirroring the sustained $\text{VAR}(4)$ under-coverage.

\subsection{Discussion of Results}

This extension asks whether dynamic misspecification gives local projections a genuine 
finite-sample advantage. In point estimation it does not, and on the complexity frontier the 
picture is one of continuity rather than repositioning: $\text{LP}(4)$ remains equivalent to a 
heavily parameterized VAR under misspecification just as under correct specification. The one 
decisive edge is again inferential.

Adding the MA component raises the absolute root mean squared error of every specification, but 
not uniformly. The low-order segment of the frontier lifts most sharply, most visibly in the 
jagged $\text{VAR}(2)$ spike at $\rho=0.95$ and $\tau=0.6$. The reason is the truncation bias of 
Section~2.1: under an MA process a finite-order VAR is generically misspecified and carries a 
population-level bias that does not vanish asymptotically 
\parencite{braun1993misspecifications,ravenna2007vector}, and this bias bites hardest at the 
parsimonious end. Because the local projection reads each horizon off a separate regression 
rather than compounding misspecified short-run dynamics through $\hat{A}^{h}$, its error inflates 
far less \parencite{olea2024double}. $\text{LP}(4)$'s standing relative to the low-order VAR 
class therefore improves, even as its own absolute error rises. The $\text{LP}(4)$ crossing 
itself, by contrast, is close to invariant: holding $T=250$ fixed it stays near $q\approx 16$ to 
$19$ across the full misspecification range and shifts by at most a single VAR order between 
$\tau=0$ and $\tau=0.6$, while persistence moves it more, from $q\approx 19$ at $\rho=0.5$ to 
$q\approx 17$ at $\rho=0.95$.

In any case the point-estimation ranking is untouched. The equal-lag $\text{VAR}(4)$ stays 
strictly below the $\text{LP}(4)$ line at every persistence level and misspecification strength, 
because at the $\tau$ considered the truncation bias is too small to offset the variance gulf 
between them. Consistent with broader evidence, a genuine bias-variance trade-off exists under 
approximable misspecification, but the VAR remains MSE-preferable unless bias reduction is 
weighted very heavily \parencite{li2024local}. The robustness of local projections to dynamic 
misspecification thus shows up only in bias, not in point-estimation accuracy.

The surviving advantage is inferential, and here too the extension mirrors the baseline. 
$\text{LP}(4)$ holds near-nominal coverage at $\rho=0.5$ and $\rho=0.7$ and slips only mildly 
near the unit root, while the $\text{VAR}(4)$ delta-method intervals stay miscalibrated and fall 
to roughly $0.74$ at $\rho=0.95$. One feature is new. At $\rho=0.95$ the gap between the 
empirical sampling dispersion and the delta-method standard error no longer closes at long 
horizons, because the MA contamination injects dispersion that a standard error built on the 
misspecified $\text{VAR}(4)$ cannot capture, compounding the near-unit-root breakdown of the 
Taylor approximation \parencite{kilian1998small,kilian1999finite} with the spuriously tight 
intervals of an underparameterized model \parencite{kilian2001impulse}. Local-projection 
inference avoids both channels, since its heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors do not 
assume a correctly specified propagation model \parencite{montiel2021local}. The edge is not 
uniform, however, as the flexible $\text{VAR}(23)$ approximates the VARMA dynamics well enough 
to match or slightly exceed $\text{LP}(4)$ coverage even at $\rho=0.95$, exactly as the 
complexity-frontier logic predicts.
