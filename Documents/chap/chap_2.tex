\section{Theoretical Background (total: 5-6.5 pages)}
Impulse response functions can be nonparametically defined as the difference between two conditional forecasts of the outcome variable (e.g. Koop (1996)). Both forecasts are conditioned on the same history up to period $t-1$, but differ in the realization at time $t$. While one conditions on a shock of size $\delta$ to the variable of interest, the other one does not. Following \textcite{ramey2016macroeconomic} shocks hereby refers to a significant exogenous force representing unanticipated movements in exogeneous variables, that is uncorrelated with other current and lagged control variables and other exogeneous shocks. The observed difference in the projected paths at horizon $h$ then identifies the causal effect of the shock in population:

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{IR}(h,\delta) \equiv \mathbb{E}\left[y_{t+h}|s_t = s_0 + \delta; \mathbf{x_t}\right] - \mathbb{E}\left[y_{t+h}|s_t = s_0; \mathbf{x_t}\right]
\end{equation}

Vector Autoregressions and Local Projections serve as two complementary  approaches to estimating impulse responses in finite samples. The former,  introduced by \textcite{sims1980macroeconomics}, parametrizes the joint  dynamics of all variables in a single autoregressive system and impulse  responses are then obtained as iterated forecasts from the estimated  companion matrix. The latter, established by \textcite{jorda2005estimation},  takes a direct forecasting approach by estimating the responses at each horizon independently via OLS, projecting the future outcome directly onto current and lagged values of the data. The two approaches compound estimation error differently across horizons and, at equal lag order, occupy opposite ends of a bias-variance spectrum in finite samples \parencites{li2024local}{olea2025local}. Local projections tend toward  higher variance, vector autoregressions toward higher bias under misspecification. 
% For unrestricted lag lengths, \textcite{plagborg2021local} establish that both methods recover the exact same impulse response function in population. 
More recently, however, \textcite{ludwig2026local} shows that the equal-order comparison is itself misleading. At a given lag order $p$, LP$(p)$ is strictly more general than VAR$(p)$, so the conventional bias-variance trade-off conflates the choice of estimator with the choice of model complexity.


\subsection{Introduction to VARS (1.5 - 2 pages)}

Following \textcite{lutkepohl2005}, we start by defining a zero mean VAR(p) model of the form where the outcome of interest, $y_t$, is a $K \times 1$ random vector, $A_i$ are fixed $K\times K$ coefficient matrices and $u_t$ are a K dimensional white noise processes with $\mathbb{E}\left[ u_t\right] = 0$, $\mathbb{E}\left[ u_tu_t{'}\right] = \Sigma_u$, i.e. being a nonsingular covariance matrix, and $\mathbb{E}\left[ u_tu_s{'}\right] = 0$ for $t\neq s$ 
%
\begin{equation}
    y_t = \nu + A_1 y_{t-1} + \dots + A_py_{t-p} + u_t, \quad t = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2,\dots
\end{equation}
%
or simply in form of the companion VAR(1)
%
\begin{equation}
    Y_t = \boldsymbol{\nu} + \mathbf{A} Y_{t-1} + U_t
\end{equation}
%
where
%
\begin{equation*}
Y_t := \begin{bmatrix} y_t \\ y_{t-1} \\ \vdots \\ y_{t-p+1} \end{bmatrix}, 
\boldsymbol{\nu} := \begin{bmatrix} \nu \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},
\mathbf{A} := \begin{bmatrix} 
A_1 & A_2 & \cdots & A_{p-1} & A_p \\ 
I_K & 0   & \cdots & 0       & 0   \\
0   & I_K &        & 0       & 0   \\
\vdots & & \ddots & & \vdots \\
0   & 0   & \cdots & I_K     & 0
\end{bmatrix},
U_t := \begin{bmatrix} u_t \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation*}
%
Assuming $Y_t$ satisfies the stability condition such that all roots, $z$, of the characteristic equation lie strictly outside the complex unit circle, the process is covariance stationary and admits a Wold moving-average representation.
%
\begin{equation}
det(I_{Kp} - \mathbf{A}z) \neq 0 \text{ for } |z| \le 1 \iff Y_t = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty}\mathbf{A}^{i}U_{t-i}
\end{equation}
%
The Wold moving-average coefficients for a specific horizon $h$, $\Psi_h \equiv [\mathbf{A}^h]$ are obtained by noting that since $U_{t-i} = (u_{t-i}, 0, \ldots, 0)'$  has nonzero entries only in its first $K$ positions. The effect of  $u_{t-i}$ on $y_t$ is therefore fully captured by the upper-left $K \times K$  block of $\mathbf{A}^i$. Extracting its upper-left $K \times K$ block thus yields  $\Psi_h$ corresponing to the impulse response of $y_t$  to a unit reduced-form innovation $h$ periods ago. 
However, since the components of $u_t$ are generally contemporaneously correlated, $\Sigma_u = \mathbb{E}(u_t u_t') \neq I_K$, a unit shock to one component of $u_t$ will in general be accompanied by simultaneous movements in the other components. Consequently, the reduced-form impulse responses $\Psi_h$ do not admit a structural interpretation. Structural  identification is only achieved by imposing restrictions on the impact matrix  $B$ in the decomposition $u_t = B\varepsilon_t$, where $\varepsilon_t  \sim (0, I_K)$ are mutually uncorrelated structural shocks. 

The most common approach is recursive Cholesky identification, which 
restricts $B$ to be lower triangular with positive diagonal entries, 
uniquely recovering $B$ as the Cholesky factor of $\Sigma_u = BB'$. 
The recursive ordering imposes a causal structure on the system: the 
variable ordered first responds contemporaneously to all shocks, while 
the variable ordered last is insulated from all contemporaneous feedback. 
Defining the orthogonalised innovations $w_t := B^{-1}u_t$ yields 
$\mathbb{E}(w_t w_t') = B^{-1}\Sigma_u (B^{-1})' = I_K$, so that the 
components of $w_t$ are mutually uncorrelated with unit variance. 
Substituting into the Wold representation gives the structural 
moving-average representation
%
\begin{equation}
    y_t = \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \Theta_j w_{t-j}, 
    \qquad \Theta_j := \Psi_j B,
    \label{eq:sma}
\end{equation}
%
where the $jk$-th element of $\Theta_h$ measures the response of variable 
$j$ to a unit innovation in the $k$-th orthogonalised shock $h$ periods 
ago. Selecting the response of variable $i$ to the $j$-th shock yields 
the structural impulse response at horizon $h$,
%
\begin{equation}
    \theta_h^{ij} = e_i'\,\Psi_h\, B\, e_j,
    \label{eq:sirf}
\end{equation}
%
where $e_i, e_j \in \mathbb{R}^K$ are standard basis vectors.
\subsubsection{POINT ESTIMATION}
Estimation of the structural impulse response $\theta_h^{ij}$ proceeds in 
two stages. In the first stage, the reduced-form VAR is estimated 
equation by equation by ordinary least squares. Since every equation 
contains the same set of regressors—a constant and $p$ lags of all 
variables—generalised least squares offers no efficiency gain over 
equation-by-equation OLS, and the two estimators coincide 
\textcite{zellner1962efficient}. Collecting the coefficients in 
$\hat{A} = (\hat{A}_1, \ldots, \hat{A}_p)$, the OLS estimator is 
consistent and asymptotically normal under the stability condition,
%
\begin{equation}
    \sqrt{T}\,\bigl(\mathrm{vec}(\hat{A}) - \mathrm{vec}(A)\bigr) 
    \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\; \Gamma^{-1} \otimes \Sigma_u\right),
\end{equation}
%
where $\Gamma = \mathbb{E}(Z_t Z_t')$ is the second-moment matrix of the 
stacked regressors $Z_t = (1, y_{t-1}', \ldots, y_{t-p}')'$ 
\textcite{lutkepohl2005}. The innovation covariance matrix is 
estimated by the residual analogue $\hat{\Sigma}_u = (T - Kp - 1)^{-1} 
\sum_{t} \hat{u}_t \hat{u}_t'$. The lag order $p$ is selected by an 
information criterion such as the AIC \textcite{akaike1974new} or the 
BIC \textcite{schwarz1978estimating}, which balance in-sample fit against 
model complexity.

In the second stage, the structural objects are recovered from the 
reduced-form estimates. The impact matrix $\hat{B}$ is obtained as the 
Cholesky factor of $\hat{\Sigma}_u$, and the reduced-form impulse 
responses $\hat{\Psi}_h$ are computed from $\hat{A}$ via the recursion
%
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\Psi}_h = \sum_{\ell=1}^{\min(h,p)} \hat{A}_\ell\, \hat{\Psi}_{h-\ell}, 
    \qquad \hat{\Psi}_0 = I_K,
\end{equation}
%
or equivalently as the upper-left block of $\hat{\mathbf{A}}^h$. The 
structural impulse response estimator is then
%
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\theta}_h^{ij} = e_i'\, \hat{\Psi}_h\, \hat{B}\, e_j,
\end{equation}
%
a smooth nonlinear function of the first-stage estimates $\hat{A}$ and 
$\hat{\Sigma}_u$.

\subsubsection*{ASYMPTOTIC INFERENCE}

Because the structural impulse response $\hat{\theta}_h^{ij}$ is a smooth 
nonlinear function of the first-stage estimates, its asymptotic 
distribution follows from the delta method. Let $\alpha = \mathrm{vec}(A_1, 
\ldots, A_p)$ and $\sigma = \mathrm{vech}(\Sigma_u)$ collect the 
reduced-form parameters, with the joint estimator satisfying
%
\begin{equation}
    \sqrt{T}
    \begin{pmatrix} \hat{\alpha} - \alpha \\ \hat{\sigma} - \sigma \end{pmatrix}
    \xrightarrow{d} 
    \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\; 
    \begin{pmatrix} \Sigma_{\alpha} & 0 \\ 0 & \Sigma_{\sigma} \end{pmatrix}
    \right).
\end{equation}
%
Since the impulse response is obtained through the differentiable mapping 
$\theta_h^{ij} = g(\alpha, \sigma)$, the delta method propagates this 
asymptotic normality through the Jacobian of $g$, yielding
%
\begin{equation}
    \sqrt{T}\,\bigl(\hat{\theta}_h^{ij} - \theta_h^{ij}\bigr) 
    \xrightarrow{d} 
    \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\; 
    \frac{\partial g}{\partial \alpha'}\,\Sigma_{\alpha}\,
    \frac{\partial g'}{\partial \alpha}
    + \frac{\partial g}{\partial \sigma'}\,\Sigma_{\sigma}\,
    \frac{\partial g'}{\partial \sigma}\right),
\end{equation}
%
where the Jacobian of the reduced-form responses 
$G_h = \partial\,\mathrm{vec}(\Psi_h)/\partial\alpha'$ has the closed form 
$G_h = \sum_{m=0}^{h-1} J(\mathbf{A}')^{h-1-m} \otimes \Psi_m$ 
\textcite{lutkepohl2005}. The resulting standard errors deliver 
confidence intervals for $\hat{\theta}_h^{ij}$ that are valid pointwise in 
the horizon $h$, provided the process is stationary with roots bounded away 
from the unit circle.

This pointwise validity comes with an important caveat. As 
\textcite{lutkepohl2005} notes, the asymptotic covariance matrix 
in the proposition can become singular for certain parameter 
configurations, in which case the usual $t$-ratios are no longer 
asymptotically standard normal and inference based on them may be 
misleading. More fundamentally, the delta-method approximation is valid 
only pointwise: it is not guaranteed to hold uniformly over the persistence 
of the process, nor at horizons that grow with the sample size 
\textbf{mikusheva2012onedimensional}. These qualifications motivate a 
closer look at the finite-sample behaviour of VAR-based inference.

\subsubsection*{Small-Sample Limitations}

The favourable asymptotic properties established above are qualified by 
three interrelated problems that arise in the sample sizes typical of 
macroeconomic applications. All three originate in the same feature that 
gives the VAR its parsimony: impulse responses are obtained by iterating 
the estimated one-step dynamics forward, so that estimation error enters 
the response nonlinearly and accumulates across horizons.

First, estimation error propagates through iteration. Because 
$\hat{\Psi}_h$ is the upper-left block of $\hat{\mathbf{A}}^h$, error in 
$\hat{A}$ enters the impulse response through powers of the companion 
matrix and is compounded multiplicatively as the horizon grows. Even small 
coefficient biases therefore translate into substantial distortions of the 
estimated responses at longer horizons \textbf{kilian1998small}. In the 
limiting case of unit or near-unit roots, \textbf{phillips1998impulse} 
shows that long-horizon impulse responses from unrestricted VARs are not 
even consistently estimated, converging instead to random variables rather 
than the true responses.

Second, the autoregressive coefficients themselves are biased downward in 
short samples. It is a long-established result that least squares 
underestimates the persistence of an autoregressive process 
\textbf{pope1990biases}, and because $\hat{\Psi}_h$ inherits this bias 
through the recursion, the estimated impulse responses decay too quickly 
and understate the true persistence of the system. The distortion is most 
severe precisely when persistence is high and the sample is short—the 
empirically relevant case for many macroeconomic series.

Third, these distortions undermine delta-method inference. When the largest 
roots approach the unit circle, the asymptotic normal approximation 
deteriorates and the resulting confidence intervals undercover, 
increasingly so at longer horizons \textbf{kilian1998small}. The failure 
is not merely a finite-sample inconvenience but reflects the non-uniformity 
of the underlying asymptotics: the normal approximation holds pointwise but 
not uniformly over the persistence parameter 
\textbf{mikusheva2012onedimensional}. The standard practical remedy is 
the bias-corrected bootstrap of \textbf{kilian1998small}, which 
bias-adjusts the estimated coefficients before resampling and thereby 
accounts for both the bias and the skewness of the small-sample impulse 
response distribution.

Taken together, these limitations—all rooted in the iterated-forecasting 
structure of the VAR—motivate the study of estimators that recover impulse 
responses directly at each horizon, without iterating a fitted one-step 
model forward. The local projection approach of the following section is 
precisely such an alternative.

\subsection{On the Equivalence of VARs and Local Projections (2-2.5 pages)}

As Local Projection inference gained in popularity over the last couple of years, more and more scholars have started comparing the asymptotical and finite sample trade-offs of both methods. Prior research has established the notion that SVARs are computational more efficient while LPs inherit favorable robustness to misspecification (see e.g.\cites{ramey2016macroeconomic}{jorda2005estimation}). The notion applies in analogy to the comparison of iterated vs. direct forcasting as established by \textcite{marcellino2006comparison}.

\textcite{plagborg2021local} were the first to actually show that under weak stationarity and unrestriced lag structure both methods estimate the identical impulse response function in population. Therefore any conventional LP impulse response can be obtrained through an appropriately ordered recursive VAR and any VAR impulse response through a LP with appropriate control variables. 
- they do not impose restrictions of the underlying DGP
The logic applies that any VAR model with sufficiently large lag lengths is able to capture the whole covariance properties of the data such that VAR($\infty$) forecasts coincide with direct LP forecasts. 
The authors come to the conclusion: 
- finite lag length, impulse response approximately agree if the same lag length is used for both but differ for $h \ge p$. 
- in population, linear local projections are exactly as robust to non-linearities in the DGP as linear VARs 

Here I will discuss the theoretical results that establish the equivalence of VARs and LPs under certain conditions, as shown by Plagborg-Moller \& Wolf (2021) and Fusari et al. (2026). I will also explain how this equivalence is affected by finite sample properties and dynamic misspecification, as discussed by Li et al. (2024), Montiel Olea et al. (2025), and Ludwig (2026).

The conventional finite-sample comparison between local projections and vector autoregressions is typically conducted at equal lag order, pitting LP(p) against VAR(p). Ludwig (2026) shows that this convention rests on a misleading notion of parsimony. His Theorem 1 establishes an exact finite-sample identity: the h-step prediction of an LP(p) is the forward iteration of a sequence of one-step VAR predictions whose lag order rises from p to $p + h - 1$. An equal-order VAR(p) is therefore a restricted special case of LP(p), obtained by holding the lag order fixed along the recursion rather than allowing it to increase. The familiar empirical pattern—lower approximation bias but higher sampling variance for LP relative to a same-order VAR—is, from this vantage point, largely a mechanical consequence of comparing a more general specification with its own restriction, rather than evidence of an intrinsic property of either estimator. This matters because it leaves the standard reading of the bias-variance trade-off, as articulated by Plagborg-Møller and Wolf (2021) and Montiel Olea et al. (2025), confounded: at equal lag order, the method effect (LP versus VAR) cannot be separated from the complexity effect (more versus fewer effective autoregressive parameters).
